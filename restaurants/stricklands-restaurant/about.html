The long version

A Southern table, three generations on.

Essie Strickland opened the doors in 1960. Her nephew Paul took over in 1975. His kids are on the line now. This is how we got here.

Essie Strickland opened her restaurant on Broad Street in Athens in 1960. Athens was a smaller town then. The football stadium was smaller. The river was closer. Broad Street had a stretch of little rooms that served breakfast to the men who worked the rail yards and the students who were paying attention. Ms. Essie's was one of them. Biscuits from the oven, coffee on a hot plate, a chalkboard that told you what was on for lunch.

She ran it for fifteen years on her own. A one-woman line most mornings, a cafeteria steam table at noon. People knew her by name. She knew what they ordered before they asked.

1975 — the second generation

Paul Strickland was thirty-six years old when he bought the restaurant from his Aunt Essie in 1975. He had worked in the kitchen on and off since he was a teenager. The handover was quiet — a new name on the business license, the same name over the door. A couple of years later, he moved the restaurant a few blocks east, to a storefront on East Broad Street just across from the UGA Arch.

That was the decade students started showing up for breakfast. The hours crept earlier. The biscuit order doubled. Paul kept Aunt Essie's recipes and added his own — the pork tenderloin plate, the cornmeal-crust catfish, the Friday fish fry that people still plan their week around.

1995 — out to Atlanta Highway

After twenty years on East Broad, downtown Athens was changing in ways that did not make room for a meat-and-three. Parking was hard. Deliveries were harder. In 1995 the restaurant moved west to 4723 Atlanta Highway, where there was a parking lot and a loading dock and room to grow. Regulars followed. So did every cook on the line. So did the bell at the register, which still rings when an order is up.

Two years later, in 1997, the building was remodeled and expanded. The dining room grew, a proper meeting room was added for church groups and Rotary lunches, and the catering operation moved into a real kitchen of its own. The menu did not change. The expansion was made to serve more of it.

We do not chase trends. We feed the people who showed up.
— Paul Strickland, second generation

The way the line works

The line has worked the same way since Ms. Essie. You walk in, you pick up a tray, you tell the first cook the meat. You point at the sides. The last cook hands you a biscuit or cornbread and a ticket with your table number. You pay at the register on the way out. It is not complicated. It has never been complicated.

Breakfast prep starts at five. Biscuits go in the oven at five-thirty. The first coffee pot goes on at five-forty-five. By six, the door is open. Lunch prep overlaps breakfast — the greens have been simmering since yesterday, the fryer is warming, the chicken has been brining overnight. At ten-thirty the steam table fills. At eleven the lunch line forms. At two we close the doors and start again.

The neighborhood keeps changing. We don't.

Athens is a college town that grew into a city. The old downtown is barely the downtown anymore. A lot of places we were neighbors with on Broad Street are gone. A lot of places that came later are also gone. We are still here because we did not try to be anything other than what we are. A breakfast and lunch restaurant. A cafeteria line. A family name over the door.

We do a lot of catering now — weddings, funerals, office lunches, UGA game-day spreads. The meeting room at the back of the restaurant gets booked months out for birthdays and anniversaries and the occasional wake. It is a good room for a gathering. It has our food in it.

Today

The third generation is on the line. The biscuit recipe is the biscuit recipe. The hours have not changed in thirty years. Monday through Friday, six in the morning to two in the afternoon. Saturday until eleven-thirty. Sunday we are home with our families, same as always. Sixty-five years in, that is the whole story.

If you are in Athens on a weekday morning, come get breakfast. Order the country ham biscuit. We will see you around.
